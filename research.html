Research Interests

My research interest is in subject indexing, which I define as a process of determining what a resource is about—subject analysis—and the subsequent process of representing that aboutness in an indexing language—subject representation.

I study and characterize how different types of subject indexers do subject indexing work; how different types of subject indexers adopt new processes and tools and whether they adopt from other types of subject indexers; and how relatively recent technological developments like large digital collections and data sets, social tagging, and machine classification and clustering influence subject indexing.

I typically study subject indexing through case studies of specific subject indexers working with specific collections, focusing on the artifacts that they create—both the indexes themselves and artifacts that reveal their processes.

I consider myself a Pragmatist when it comes to subject indexing, focusing on workable solutions that help people find information.

Theoretical Foundations of Knowledge Organization

My research considers the place of subject indexing in the broader field of knowledge organization. Knowledge organization, which is grounded in foundational works by Cutter, Bliss, Broadfield, Wilson, Svenonious, Hjørland and others, begins in bibliographic classification and library studies, but includes subject indexing for all types of collections. The dominant type of subject indexers in this field are professional indexers, such as librarians and archivists, who developed best practices and established the theoretical foundations of the field through such works as Two Kinds of Power by Patrick Wilson and Subject analysis: principles and procedures by D.W. Langridge. Professional indexers also laid the foundation for indexing languages, such as DDC, LCC, Ranganathan's analytico-synthetic Colon Classification, and more modern representations such as the Semantic Web and ontologies. My research also considers more recent foundational studies in subject indexing, such as information seeking (Beghtol), social and ethical implications of indexes (Furner, Adler), and how subject indexing fits with other theories and frameworks (Mai).

Types of Subject Indexers in Action

Studying different types of subject indexers in action is the heart of my research. I study the motivations, processes, and tools of four types of subject indexers:

  • Professional indexers, such as librarians and archivists.
  • Domain experts and folk experts, such as the folk biologists described by Dupré and the domain experts that built the Gene Ontology (Smith)
  • Casual indexers and social taggers, such as the widely studied internet users who contribute to folksonomies and parents categorizing a classroom book collection.
  • Machine indexers that classify and cluster information, such as Word2vec, which determines the linguistic context of words and places them "close" to words with similar contexts
I study these different types of subject indexers and the actions that they take as they analyze information objects and determine how to represent them.

My research aims to answer three types of questions about subject indexers:

Comparison of types of subject indexers
How do different types of subject indexers compare in their motivations, biases tools, processes, and evaluation tech? Researchers like Adler, Bates and Rowley, Kipp, and others have done good research in this space, but I think that we can learn more, especially about domain experts and machines, through direct comparison of the indexes that different subject indexers produce and notes about their processes and decision-making. We can also learn about these other subject indexers through studies that observe and describe their processes, tools, and outputs but do not explicitly compare them other types of subject indexers.

Hybrid Subject Indexing Approaches
Do subject indexers adopt tools and processes that were initially developed by other types of subject indexers? For example, many libraries have incorporated social tags into their catalogs (Maness, Furner). How did they learn about and evaluate that technology? What were the barriers to adoption? When is adopting tools and processes from other subject indexers most fruitful? Can we use this knowledge to build hybrid subject indexing approaches? I believe that these questions of adoption are especially interesting with the emergence of digital technology and very large digital collections. In particular, I want to emphasize the impact of social tagging (Golder and Huberman, Munk and Mork, and others), machine indexers (De Campos, et al., Golub, Mikolov et al., and others), and digital cataloging and indexing tools (Soergel) on subject indexing in this emergent digital environment.

Subject Indexing at Social, Cultural, and Ethical Intersections
How do the motivations, processes, and tools of different types of subject indexers intersect with social, cultural, and ethical aspects of subject indexing? Can folksonomies represent minority voices? Can machine indexers overcome biases in their training data? How do subject indexers promote social justice, and how do the answers to that question change depending on the type of subject indexer? How can indexes reflect and honor both mainstream and folk wisdom? Because indexes codify the views of subject indexers in persistent and powerful ways, understanding how different subject indexers arrive at their views and representation choices is critical.

Social Tagging and Folksonomies

So far, I have researched social and casual indexers more than other types of indexers. I loved the old tagging service de.licio.us and am fascinated by any social tagging website, including MetaFilter, Stack Overflow, Flickr, and Goodreads.

In these folksonomies, the crowd collectively determines what something is and where it goes. Folksonomies usually lack hierarchy, and new metadata is added on an ongoing basis, which makes for adaptable and flexible classification schemes. Of course, the crowd isn't always "right" and isn't always organized, but that's part of what makes social tagging and folksonomies so interesting.

I don't just observe social tagging in wild; I also build tagging tools. I built tag-based navigation and retrieval tools for Google's internal documentation that allowed Google engineers to define terms and tag key resources. As a researcher, I have studied people's tagging behaviors in controlled experiments. You can use the experimental taggging user interface that I built for some of my research.

About

I am a PhD candidate at the University of Washington's Information School.

My research is in knowledge organization, subject indexing, and social tagging. I am particularly interested in comparing and combining the subject indexing approaches of professional indexers, domain and folk experts, social and casual indexers, and machine indexers.
